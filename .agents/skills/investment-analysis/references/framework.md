# Framework From The Book

This reference distills the investment ideas needed for analysis. It is a working framework, not a literary summary.

## First Principles

- Invest with an owner/operator mindset. A public security is a claim on a real business or basket of businesses, not a ticker pattern.
- Long-term return comes from cash flow, business quality, and price paid.
- Valuation is an odds system. Cheap assets can keep falling and expensive assets can keep rising, but the entry price shapes long-term payoff and tolerance for error.
- Prefer verifiable facts over distant growth stories. Growth matters only when protected by barriers and converted into cash.
- The best opportunities often appear when the market overreacts to exposed, widely discussed bad news, but contrarian investing needs evidence, not attitude.

## The Three Questions

Every stock, ETF, or index report should answer:

1. Why is it cheap or expensive?
   - Current valuation vs history.
   - Current valuation vs peers.
   - Current valuation vs normalized profitability.
   - Market concerns that explain the discount or premium.

2. Why is it good or bad?
   - Business model or index methodology.
   - Moat, pricing power, concentration, cash flow quality.
   - Whether the asset contains winners or weak participants.

3. Why now?
   - Whether bad news is priced in.
   - Whether policy, liquidity, earnings, and sentiment are improving or deteriorating.
   - Whether there is enough margin of safety before obvious catalysts appear.

## Three-Part Core: Valuation, Quality, Timing

Use the book's simplified frame before adding detail:

- Valuation is the most learnable part. Compare price with current value, normalized value, history, peers, and pessimism already embedded in the quote.
- Quality is the part that deserves the most work. Identify the industry rule that determines who wins, then decide whether the company or index holds those winners.
- Timing is useful but unreliable. Treat catalysts, cycles, and liquidity as supporting evidence, not as a substitute for buying a good asset at a reasonable or cheap price.

Translate this into "good industry, good company, good price":

- Good industry: high or rising concentration, supply discipline, visible winners, durable demand, regulatory structure that does not destroy returns, and bargaining power over suppliers or customers.
- Good company: clear moat, pricing power, repeatable growth, cash conversion, management discipline, and a balance sheet that can survive adverse scenarios.
- Good price: valuation low enough against normalized earnings, assets, cash flow, dividend capacity, or peer opportunity cost that reasonable mistakes do not destroy the thesis.

Prefer simple, durable variables over fragile forecasts. The analysis should explain the industry law, business model, and cash flow first; only then discuss narrative, theme, or short-term catalyst.

## Scorecard

Use scoring to make judgment auditable. It is a discipline aid, not a trading system.

General scoring rules:

- Score each category from evidence. Do not give full credit for unverified claims.
- Penalize missing data when the missing field can change the conclusion.
- Mention the top three deductions in the report.
- Apply vetoes before the final label. A veto caps the decision at Watchlist or Avoid even if the total score is high.
- Treat 80-100 as strong, 65-79 as acceptable but conditional, 50-64 as mixed/watchlist, and below 50 as weak or avoid. Override the band when risk asymmetry clearly demands it.

ETF/index scorecard, 100 points:

| Category | Points | What To Check |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Valuation | 25 | PE/PB percentile, dividend yield, valuation vs ROE, history window, peer comparison, earnings-cycle adjustment |
| Exposure quality | 25 | leader weight, "moon" content, concentration, pricing power, cash-flow quality, industry structure |
| Index method and wrapper | 15 | methodology, rebalance behavior, theme purity, fee, liquidity, tracking error, premium/discount, fund size |
| Timing and cycle | 10 | policy, liquidity, market valuation, economic cycle, earnings cycle, crowding and flows |
| Margin of safety and risk | 15 | downside support, value/growth/cycle traps, reflexivity, liquidity mismatch, forced-sale risk |
| Data confidence | 10 | source freshness, cross-source agreement, coverage of constituents, missing fields and provider failures |

Stock scorecard, 100 points:

| Category | Points | What To Check |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Valuation | 25 | current multiple vs history and peers, normalized earnings, asset value, free cash flow, dividend support |
| Business and industry quality | 30 | moat, pricing power, repeatable growth, industry structure, bargaining power, management and capital allocation |
| Financial quality | 15 | ROE/ROIC source, margin stability, cash conversion, working capital, capex, debt, dilution |
| Timing and catalyst | 10 | bad news priced in, policy/liquidity, earnings inflection, sentiment, but with humility about timing |
| Margin of safety and risk | 15 | downside scenario, reflexivity, balance sheet, accounting risk, value/growth/cycle traps |
| Data confidence | 5 | latest filings, source consistency, missing three-statement or governance fields |

Hard vetoes:

- The business, index, or technology path is outside the ability boundary and cannot be valued with confidence.
- Current valuation relies on peak-cycle earnings, stale constituents, or unverified one-off profit.
- The main risk is hidden leverage, fraud, refinancing failure, forced liquidation, or reflexivity.
- The thesis requires a precise future winner in a fragmented or fast-changing industry.
- Cash flow, debt, pledge, or governance data is missing and those fields are central to the decision.

## Quality Signals

Use these signals to judge a business or the weighted businesses inside an index:

- Pricing power: ability to raise or hold prices without losing customers.
- Barriers: brand, license, resource, patent, scale, network effect, switching cost, cost advantage, regional dominance.
- Cash conversion: profit turns into operating cash flow without persistent receivable, inventory, or capex drag.
- Concentration: industries with dominant leaders or clear oligopoly tend to have better economics than endless fragmented competition.
- Replicability: growth does not destroy economics as the company expands.
- Management and incentives: strategy, governance, capital allocation, and operating discipline preserve the moat.

## ETF And Index Translation

An ETF is a wrapper. Analyze both wrapper and exposure.

Wrapper:

- Fund code, name, type, exchange, manager.
- Target index and tracking method.
- Expense ratio, tracking error, premium/discount, liquidity, size.
- Latest NAV, intraday estimate, historical NAV.

Exposure:

- Index valuation: PE, PB, valuation percentiles, dividend yield, ROE, PEG.
- Top holdings, top-10 concentration, sector concentration.
- Whether the index holds "moons": a small number of advantaged companies with visible winner status, durable profit pools, and hard-to-replicate competitive positions. Do not count many speculative "stars" as equivalent.
- Whether earnings are currently depressed, normal, or at a cycle peak.
- Whether methodology creates quality exposure or simply theme exposure.

Index quality checks:

- Leader weight: how much of the index is in companies that already have scale, brand, cost, license, network, resource, or channel advantages.
- Profit distribution: whether index-level ROE comes from many healthy constituents or from a few cyclical outliers.
- Industry structure: whether concentration is rising, stable, or falling; fragmented industries need lower conviction.
- Structure label: classify the exposure as clear leader, two strong leaders, several strong leaders, fragmented price war, or winner not yet knowable.
- Methodology behavior: whether rebalancing rewards quality and durability or mechanically chases recent winners.
- Earnings position: whether current PE is low because profits are temporarily high, temporarily low, or normal.
- Theme quality: whether the theme has proven business models and cash flow, not only policy support or large addressable market.

Decision lens:

- A low-valuation index can be attractive when constituent quality is acceptable, earnings are not peak-cycle, and the risks are already visible.
- A high-valuation index can be acceptable only when quality, durability, and growth runway are unusually strong.
- A theme ETF is risky when the theme is popular, constituents have unproven economics, and valuation already embeds success.

## Stock Translation

Analyze a stock as a business first.

Business:

- What does the company sell and who pays?
- Why does the customer buy from this company?
- Who has bargaining power: suppliers, customers, platforms, regulators, competitors?
- What is the real competitive boundary?

Financials:

- Revenue growth and its source.
- Gross margin and net margin stability.
- ROE/ROIC source: margin, turnover, leverage.
- Operating cash flow vs net profit.
- Capex, working capital, debt maturity, equity dilution.

Valuation:

- PE for stable earnings, but normalize cyclical profits.
- PB for financials and asset-heavy businesses, but adjust for asset quality.
- EV/EBIT or EV/EBITDA for operating businesses where capital structure matters.
- PS only when margins and terminal economics are made explicit.
- Dividend yield only when payout durability is supported by cash flow.

## Risk And Trap Checklist

Value traps:

- Low PE caused by peak-cycle earnings.
- Technology or channel disruption destroys past moat.
- Non-leaders lose share in an industry that is concentrating.
- Heavy assets, weak demand, and excess supply keep returns low.
- Reported profit does not match cash, receivables, inventory, or debt.
- Governance or incentives consume business value.

Growth traps:

- High growth with no barrier, no pricing power, or no cash conversion.
- Subsidy-driven scale with weak unit economics.
- Big addressable market but unclear winning company.
- Technology route or standard is unsettled.
- Expansion requires more capital than the business can generate.
- Market extrapolates past high growth into a saturated future.

False risk vs true risk:

- Price volatility is not automatically permanent loss.
- Hidden, unpriced risk is more dangerous than visible, priced-in risk.
- For leveraged or externally financed companies, volatility can become permanent loss through reflexivity.

Stop-loss and sell discipline:

- For value investing, the core question is whether the analysis is wrong, not whether the position is below cost.
- Sell or avoid when the business is worse than assumed, the price no longer leaves value, or a better and cheaper opportunity exists.
- Do not average down unless value traps are ruled out, margin of safety is large, and price decline does not damage the business through reflexivity.
- The user's cost basis is irrelevant to current expected return.

## Cycle Framework

Track four cycles separately:

- Policy cycle.
- Market and valuation cycle.
- Economic cycle.
- Earnings cycle.

Typical bottom sequence:

1. Policy bottom.
2. Market bottom.
3. Economic bottom.
4. Earnings bottom.

Implications:

- At a bottom, micro earnings often still look poor; use valuation, policy, and liquidity to interpret the setup.
- At a top, strong earnings can hide valuation and cycle risk.
- Financial leverage, operating leverage, and valuation leverage benefit different phases.

Use the three leverage types carefully:

- Financial leverage usually reacts early when policy and liquidity improve, but it also raises permanent-loss risk when refinancing is constrained.
- Operating leverage can create strong profit rebounds in cyclical recoveries, yet many high operating-leverage industries are structurally weak and require exit discipline.
- Valuation leverage works when expectations are too low; it fails when the starting multiple already discounts the good news.

## Margin Of Safety

Margin of safety is not a slogan; it is the answer to "what if I am wrong?"

Check:

- Valuation anchor: current assets, normalized earnings, free cash flow, dividend capacity, replacement cost, or peer opportunity cost.
- Downside support: what valuation and earnings assumptions still avoid permanent loss.
- Optionality: whether several independent paths can make the investment work, instead of requiring one perfect forecast.
- Balance sheet: whether debt, pledges, customer concentration, redemption pressure, or funding needs can force a bad sale.
- Reflexivity: whether falling prices can damage credit, customers, suppliers, employees, regulators, or collateral value.
- Positioning implication: when evidence is incomplete, prefer staged action or watchlist status over a binary positive call.

## Behavior And Ability Boundary

The book repeatedly warns that simple principles are hard to execute because psychology interferes. Add a brief behavior check when the user asks for a buy/sell view or when the asset is highly emotional.

- Ability boundary: mark businesses, technologies, or policy paths that cannot be estimated with confidence; "cannot value" is a valid conclusion.
- Anchoring: do not use the previous high, recent fall, or the user's cost as evidence of cheapness.
- Rear-view mirror: do not extrapolate the last style, asset class, or hot theme into the next cycle.
- "This time is different": require stronger evidence when the argument asks you to ignore long valuation history, cycles, or business economics.
- Crowd consensus: extreme agreement can be useful only after checking whether the consensus is already priced in.

## Decision Labels

Use these labels when the user needs a conclusion:

- Attractive: valuation, quality, timing, and risk are aligned enough to justify close attention or staged action.
- Watchlist: quality is acceptable but valuation, timing, or missing data blocks conviction.
- Neutral: risk/reward is balanced or highly dependent on uncertain assumptions.
- Avoid: valuation is poor, quality is weak, risk is underpriced, or the asset resembles a value/growth/cycle trap.

Positive-view preconditions:

- The asset is inside the analyst's ability boundary or the unknowns are explicitly bounded.
- The business, industry, or index exposure is understandable from real economics, not only market narrative.
- Quality is acceptable: moat, cash flow, concentration, pricing power, or index methodology support durable returns.
- Price is reasonable or cheap against normalized value, not merely down from a prior high.
- The main risks are visible and at least partly priced; hidden leverage, reflexivity, fraud, or disruption is not the dominant risk.
- There is a concrete margin-of-safety bridge from downside, base, and upside scenarios to the decision label.

Always include the evidence that would change the label.
