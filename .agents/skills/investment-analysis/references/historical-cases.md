# Historical Cases From The Book

Use these cases as pattern libraries, not as deterministic precedents. They help frame questions for ETF/index and stock analysis, but the current data must still drive the conclusion.

## How To Use Cases

- Pick only the cases that match the asset's industry, cycle, valuation, or risk pattern.
- State the analogy and the difference. A weak analogy is more dangerous than no analogy.
- Use cases to generate checks: what data would confirm or disprove the pattern?
- Never argue that an asset should rise because a past case rose.

## Case Library

| Case | Principle | Analysis Use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| White goods after industry consolidation | Growth alone was less important than concentration, scale, channel, cost, and brand after weaker players exited. | For appliance, manufacturing, and industry ETFs, check whether industry growth is slowing while leader economics improve. |
| Black goods and fast technology shifts | Frequent technology route changes can flatten old moats and make leaders hard to value. | Penalize fast-changing hardware, media, software, or theme indices when winners are not yet stable. |
| Mobile games and social games | Large user base and rapid growth did not guarantee durability when user stickiness, platform bargaining power, and content lifecycle were weak. | For high-growth themes, ask who owns the customer, who captures economics, and whether cash flow survives a hit-product cycle. |
| Film production companies | A popular end market can still have poor cash flow, high upfront spend, uncertain payoff, and weak pricing power versus talent or channels. | Use for media, entertainment, and project-based businesses; do not score TAM as quality without cash conversion. |
| Internet finance versus banks | Internet distribution did not automatically defeat capital, trust, regulation, offline relationship, and balance-sheet advantages. | For fintech and platform themes, separate user experience disruption from profit-pool capture. |
| 2013 theme mania versus banks and real estate leaders | Hot concepts attracted capital while low-valued traditional leaders with stronger economics were ignored. | When crowding is extreme, compare valuation and quality across hated versus loved sectors. |
| Food safety and baijiu events | Temporary, visible bad news can create opportunity when the business model and leader status remain intact. | For consumer leaders, separate temporary demand shock from permanent trust or regulation damage. |
| Bank versus pharma valuation comparison | A higher-growth industry can underperform when valuation embeds too much optimism and regulation compresses economics. | Compare expected growth already priced in, not just industry narrative. |
| Small-cap premium before 2011-2012 underperformance | A broad style can become unsafe when relative valuation reaches extremes and relies on passing the last baton. | For style ETFs, check relative valuation, crowding, and whether constituents have real economics. |
| Engineering machinery in a multi-option recovery | Margin of safety can come from several independent positive paths, not one precise forecast. | In cyclical recovery cases, list independent drivers and verify whether at least one can protect downside. |
| Steel and shipping rebounds | High operating leverage can create sharp short-term rallies in structurally weak industries, but long holding risk remains high. | For cyclical ETFs and stocks, distinguish tradeable rebound from durable investment quality. |
| Financial real estate in early policy easing | Low valuation and pricing power can create better risk/reward than higher-beta cyclicals when recovery strength is uncertain. | Use four-cycle analysis: policy bottom, market bottom, economic bottom, earnings bottom. |
| Baijiu at low valuation after anti-corruption pressure | Strong brand, clear industry structure, pricing power, and low valuation can outweigh temporary pessimism. | For premium consumer assets, verify segmentation, real competitors, channel inventory, and normalized demand. |
| Real estate leader versus film company comparison | Cash-flow model and bargaining power can matter more than market excitement or reported revenue size. | Compare who receives cash first, who finances whom, and who has the stronger contract terms. |
| Technology bubbles and venture-style payoff | A few huge winners can hide many failures; public-market investors often overpay for the visible dream. | For emerging technology baskets, demand proof of winner selection, economics, and valuation discipline. |
| Cost-basis anchoring and loss aversion | The decision to hold, sell, or add should not depend on whether the investor is above or below cost. | When the user mentions cost or break-even, redirect to valuation, quality, timing, and opportunity cost. |

## Principle-To-Case Map

- Valuation: bank vs pharma, small-cap premium, baijiu at low valuation, 2013 theme mania.
- Quality and moat: white goods, baijiu, internet finance vs banks, real estate leader vs film company.
- Industry structure: white goods, black goods, technology bubbles, mobile games.
- Cash flow and pricing power: film production, real estate leader, premium consumer brands.
- Contrarian investing: food safety events, 2013 theme mania, baijiu after anti-corruption pressure.
- Value traps: steel and shipping, fragmented heavy industries, low PE at peak-cycle profit.
- Growth traps: mobile games, internet finance overreach, technology bubbles.
- Cycle framework: financial real estate, engineering machinery, steel and shipping.
- Behavioral errors: small-cap premium, cost-basis anchoring, "this time is different" bubble narratives.
