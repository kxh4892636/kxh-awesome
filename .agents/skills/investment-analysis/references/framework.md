# Framework From The Book

This reference distills the investment ideas needed for analysis. It is a working framework, not a literary summary.

## First Principles

- Invest with an owner/operator mindset. A public security is a claim on a real business or basket of businesses, not a ticker pattern.
- Long-term return comes from cash flow, business quality, and price paid.
- Valuation is an odds system. Cheap assets can keep falling and expensive assets can keep rising, but the entry price shapes long-term payoff and tolerance for error.
- Prefer verifiable facts over distant growth stories. Growth matters only when protected by barriers and converted into cash.
- The best opportunities often appear when the market overreacts to exposed, widely discussed bad news, but contrarian investing needs evidence, not attitude.

## The Three Questions

Every stock, ETF, or index report should answer:

1. Why is it cheap or expensive?
   - Current valuation vs history.
   - Current valuation vs peers.
   - Current valuation vs normalized profitability.
   - Market concerns that explain the discount or premium.

2. Why is it good or bad?
   - Business model or index methodology.
   - Moat, pricing power, concentration, cash flow quality.
   - Whether the asset contains winners or weak participants.

3. Why now?
   - Whether bad news is priced in.
   - Whether policy, liquidity, earnings, and sentiment are improving or deteriorating.
   - Whether there is enough margin of safety before obvious catalysts appear.

## Quality Signals

Use these signals to judge a business or the weighted businesses inside an index:

- Pricing power: ability to raise or hold prices without losing customers.
- Barriers: brand, license, resource, patent, scale, network effect, switching cost, cost advantage, regional dominance.
- Cash conversion: profit turns into operating cash flow without persistent receivable, inventory, or capex drag.
- Concentration: industries with dominant leaders or clear oligopoly tend to have better economics than endless fragmented competition.
- Replicability: growth does not destroy economics as the company expands.
- Management and incentives: strategy, governance, capital allocation, and operating discipline preserve the moat.

## ETF And Index Translation

An ETF is a wrapper. Analyze both wrapper and exposure.

Wrapper:

- Fund code, name, type, exchange, manager.
- Target index and tracking method.
- Expense ratio, tracking error, premium/discount, liquidity, size.
- Latest NAV, intraday estimate, historical NAV.

Exposure:

- Index valuation: PE, PB, valuation percentiles, dividend yield, ROE, PEG.
- Top holdings, top-10 concentration, sector concentration.
- Whether the index holds "moons": dominant companies in markets where the winner is visible.
- Whether earnings are currently depressed, normal, or at a cycle peak.
- Whether methodology creates quality exposure or simply theme exposure.

Decision lens:

- A low-valuation index can be attractive when constituent quality is acceptable, earnings are not peak-cycle, and the risks are already visible.
- A high-valuation index can be acceptable only when quality, durability, and growth runway are unusually strong.
- A theme ETF is risky when the theme is popular, constituents have unproven economics, and valuation already embeds success.

## Stock Translation

Analyze a stock as a business first.

Business:

- What does the company sell and who pays?
- Why does the customer buy from this company?
- Who has bargaining power: suppliers, customers, platforms, regulators, competitors?
- What is the real competitive boundary?

Financials:

- Revenue growth and its source.
- Gross margin and net margin stability.
- ROE/ROIC source: margin, turnover, leverage.
- Operating cash flow vs net profit.
- Capex, working capital, debt maturity, equity dilution.

Valuation:

- PE for stable earnings, but normalize cyclical profits.
- PB for financials and asset-heavy businesses, but adjust for asset quality.
- EV/EBIT or EV/EBITDA for operating businesses where capital structure matters.
- PS only when margins and terminal economics are made explicit.
- Dividend yield only when payout durability is supported by cash flow.

## Risk And Trap Checklist

Value traps:

- Low PE caused by peak-cycle earnings.
- Technology or channel disruption destroys past moat.
- Non-leaders lose share in an industry that is concentrating.
- Heavy assets, weak demand, and excess supply keep returns low.
- Reported profit does not match cash, receivables, inventory, or debt.
- Governance or incentives consume business value.

Growth traps:

- High growth with no barrier, no pricing power, or no cash conversion.
- Subsidy-driven scale with weak unit economics.
- Big addressable market but unclear winning company.
- Technology route or standard is unsettled.
- Expansion requires more capital than the business can generate.
- Market extrapolates past high growth into a saturated future.

False risk vs true risk:

- Price volatility is not automatically permanent loss.
- Hidden, unpriced risk is more dangerous than visible, priced-in risk.
- For leveraged or externally financed companies, volatility can become permanent loss through reflexivity.

## Cycle Framework

Track four cycles separately:

- Policy cycle.
- Market and valuation cycle.
- Economic cycle.
- Earnings cycle.

Typical bottom sequence:

1. Policy bottom.
2. Market bottom.
3. Economic bottom.
4. Earnings bottom.

Implications:

- At a bottom, micro earnings often still look poor; use valuation, policy, and liquidity to interpret the setup.
- At a top, strong earnings can hide valuation and cycle risk.
- Financial leverage, operating leverage, and valuation leverage benefit different phases.

## Decision Labels

Use these labels when the user needs a conclusion:

- Attractive: valuation, quality, timing, and risk are aligned enough to justify close attention or staged action.
- Watchlist: quality is acceptable but valuation, timing, or missing data blocks conviction.
- Neutral: risk/reward is balanced or highly dependent on uncertain assumptions.
- Avoid: valuation is poor, quality is weak, risk is underpriced, or the asset resembles a value/growth/cycle trap.

Always include the evidence that would change the label.
